ABSTRACT

This study focuses on the design and development of a Web-Based Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer for Interference Detection. The system is built using a Raspberry Pi with a TP-Link Wi-Fi adapter capable of monitor mode, combined with a Flask-based web dashboard. The analyzer detects wireless networks in the surrounding environment, measures signal strength, identifies channel congestion, and provides recommendations for optimal channel selection.

The system addresses common issues in Wi-Fi networks, particularly interference from devices operating in the 2.4 GHz band. These devices include Bluetooth transmitters, microwave ovens, cordless phones, and neighboring Wi-Fi networks. By scanning and analyzing the wireless spectrum, the tool helps users identify the best channels to use for their networks.

The hardware setup includes a Raspberry Pi 4 connected to a TP-Link Archer T2U Plus USB Wi-Fi adapter. The adapter supports monitor mode, which allows the system to capture all wireless traffic in the area without connecting to a specific network. The software interface displays detected networks in real-time, showing their channel, signal strength, and security settings.

The dashboard provides a graphical view of channel usage, making it easy to spot congested frequencies. It also generates recommendations based on current interference levels, suggesting which channels would provide better performance. The system runs continuously and updates automatically, requiring minimal user intervention.

Testing showed that the analyzer successfully detected all nearby networks and accurately measured their signal strength. The recommendations provided by the system aligned with expected outcomes based on the number of competing networks. This tool can be useful for home users, small office administrators, and students learning about wireless networking.

CHAPTER 1

INTRODUCTION

Background of the Study

Wi-Fi networks have become essential in modern life, supporting communication, education, entertainment, and business operations in homes, schools, and offices. As more devices depend on wireless connections, the radio frequency spectrum has become increasingly crowded with competing signals. This crowding causes interference that leads to slower speeds, dropped connections, and poor user experience. Understanding and diagnosing Wi-Fi interference has become important for anyone who relies on wireless connectivity.

The 2.4 GHz frequency band is the most commonly used spectrum for Wi-Fi communication. This band is shared not only by Wi-Fi routers and computers but also by many other devices. Bluetooth speakers, microwave ovens, cordless phones, baby monitors, and wireless cameras all operate on frequencies that overlap with Wi-Fi. When these devices are active, they can disrupt wireless signals and cause noticeable performance problems. Users often experience slow internet speeds, video buffering, and connection drops without understanding why their network is underperforming.

In environments where many wireless devices coexist, interference has become a persistent challenge. Apartment buildings, office complexes, and school campuses often have dozens of Wi-Fi networks competing for the same channels. Add to this the growing number of IoT devices, wireless peripherals, and smart home gadgets, and the result is a complex RF environment where Wi-Fi signals must compete with multiple sources of interference. This situation is particularly challenging in the 2.4 GHz band, where only three non-overlapping channels (1, 6, and 11) exist for use.

Most users do not have access to professional tools that can identify interference sources. Enterprise-grade Wi-Fi analysis equipment costs hundreds or thousands of dollars, putting it out of reach for average consumers, students, and small businesses. Basic Wi-Fi scanner applications available on smartphones can display nearby networks, but they do not explain why connections are slow or which channels would provide better performance. There is a clear need for an affordable, accessible tool that can help users diagnose their Wi-Fi problems and make informed decisions about network configuration.

In partial fulfillment of the requirements for the degree of Bachelor of Science in Computer Engineering under the Expanded Tertiary Education Equivalency and Accreditation Program at Manuel S. Enverga University Foundation, this study develops a Web-Based Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer for Interference Detection. The system is designed to provide accessible Wi-Fi diagnostics using affordable hardware and open-source software, making spectrum analysis available to users who would otherwise not have access to such tools.

The analyzer is built using a Raspberry Pi single-board computer connected to a TP-Link Archer T2U Plus USB Wi-Fi adapter. The TP-Link adapter supports monitor mode, allowing the system to capture all wireless traffic within range without needing to connect to any specific network. This capability enables comprehensive scanning of the local Wi-Fi environment, collecting data from all detected networks regardless of their security settings.

The system continuously scans for nearby Wi-Fi networks and records their properties. For each detected network, the analyzer captures the network name (SSID), access point address (BSSID), channel number, signal strength (measured in dBm), and security settings. The scanning runs automatically at regular intervals, allowing the system to track changes in the wireless environment over time and identify patterns in channel usage and congestion.

The data collected is processed to calculate interference levels for each channel. The system considers both the number of networks on each channel and their signal strengths. It generates an interference score that reflects how crowded each frequency is. Higher scores indicate more interference and worse expected network performance. Based on this analysis, the system provides specific recommendations for which channels would offer the best connectivity.

Results are displayed through a web-based dashboard running on the Raspberry Pi. Users access the dashboard by opening a web browser on any device connected to the same network. The interface presents detected networks organized by frequency band, showing signal strength and channel information for each network. This real-time display allows users to observe how their Wi-Fi environment changes throughout the day.

The dashboard includes channel heatmap visualizations for both the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands. The 2.4 GHz heatmap displays all thirteen available channels with color-coded bars indicating congestion levels, making it easy to spot which frequencies are most crowded. A separate visualization shows the 5 GHz band for users with dual-band routers. These graphics help users quickly understand which channels to avoid and which might provide better performance.

The recommendation panel provides actionable advice based on current scan data. It displays the recommended channel for both frequency bands along with the current interference level. The system also offers practical tips for improving Wi-Fi performance based on the specific conditions detected in the user's environment. This guidance is presented in clear language that users without technical backgrounds can understand and apply.

A latency monitoring feature allows users to check network responsiveness directly from the dashboard. The system displays ping results to both the local router and an external internet server. This helps users determine whether slow connections are caused by local interference or broader network issues. Color-coded indicators show whether latency is within acceptable ranges, providing quick feedback on network health.

The system is configured to start automatically when the Raspberry Pi boots up. A systemd service manages the analyzer, ensuring continuous operation without requiring manual intervention. The compact, portable hardware setup allows the analyzer to be deployed in different locations for site surveys, making it useful for checking Wi-Fi conditions throughout a home, office, or campus.

The entire system is built using open-source software to minimize costs. The Raspberry Pi runs Raspberry Pi OS Lite, a lightweight Linux distribution suitable for headless operation. The scanning functionality uses airodump-ng from the aircrack-ng package, a well-established tool in wireless analysis. The web interface is powered by Flask, a Python web framework that handles server-side operations efficiently. This combination of free tools keeps the project affordable while delivering reliable performance.

Testing demonstrated that the analyzer successfully detected all networks in the test environment. Signal strength measurements matched expectations based on distance from each access point, and the channel recommendations aligned with observed congestion levels. The dashboard operated continuously without crashes or significant memory issues, confirming its stability for extended use. These results validate that the system can serve its intended purpose as a practical Wi-Fi diagnostic tool.

This study demonstrates how affordable hardware and open-source software can be combined to build a useful diagnostic tool for wireless networking. The Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer fills the gap between expensive commercial spectrum analyzers and basic phone applications, providing meaningful data that helps users understand their wireless environment and improve their network configuration.

4.4 Scope and Limitations of the Study

Scope

This study focuses on the design and development of a Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer that integrates both hardware and software components to deliver a comprehensive evaluation of wireless network performance. The system is engineered to detect nearby Wi-Fi networks, measure their signal strength, analyze channel congestion, and provide recommendations for optimal channel selection. These functions are achieved through continuous scanning using monitor mode on a dedicated Wi-Fi adapter.

The hardware consists of a Raspberry Pi 4 connected to a TP-Link Archer T2U Plus USB Wi-Fi adapter. This adapter supports monitor mode, which enables the system to capture all wireless traffic within range without associating with any specific network. The software runs on Raspberry Pi OS Lite and includes scanning tools from the aircrack-ng package along with a custom Flask web application for data display.

The software interface displays detected networks in real-time, organized by frequency band (2.4 GHz and 5 GHz). It includes channel heatmaps that visualize congestion levels across all available channels. A recommendation panel suggests optimal channels based on current interference analysis. The dashboard also shows latency measurements to help users assess network responsiveness.

The system is configured to run automatically when the Raspberry Pi starts, using a systemd service for reliable boot-time initialization. This allows the analyzer to function as a continuously monitoring device. The compact hardware makes it portable for deployment in different locations for site surveys. The system emphasizes modularity and scalability, allowing future enhancements such as additional visualizations, historical data logging, or integration with cloud-based analytics.

Limitations

While the Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer offers valuable capabilities for measuring and visualizing network performance, several limitations must be acknowledged to define the boundaries of its applicability. First, the system is not designed to perform enterprise-level packet decoding or advanced encryption analysis. This means it cannot be used for comprehensive network security assessments or forensic investigations. The analyzer focuses on physical and performance-layer metrics rather than deep packet inspection or protocol-level diagnostics. As a result, its use is best suited for educational and experimental purposes rather than professional-grade security auditing.

Second, the accuracy of the measurements may be influenced by environmental factors that are beyond the control of the system. Physical obstructions such as walls, furniture, and human movement can affect signal propagation and introduce variability in the data. Electromagnetic noise from nearby devices, fluctuations in temperature, and changes in humidity may also impact the reliability of the readings. The distance between the analyzer and the access point or client devices can alter the observed metrics, especially in dynamic or crowded environments. These factors must be considered when interpreting the results, and users should be aware that the tool provides indicative rather than absolute measurements.

Third, the scope of the study is confined to single-location monitoring and small-scale deployments. The prototype is designed for use in environments where variables can be managed and interference sources can be identified systematically. While this approach allows for practical evaluation, it does not fully capture the complexity of large-scale or heterogeneous network infrastructures. Real-world networks often involve multiple access points, roaming clients, and diverse traffic patterns that may not be adequately represented. Therefore, the findings and recommendations derived from the analyzer should be viewed as preliminary and context-specific.

Despite these limitations, the Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer remains a valuable resource for understanding and improving wireless network performance. It provides a foundation for further development and refinement, and its modular design allows for future upgrades that could address some of the current constraints. The system encourages hands-on learning, supports experimental validation, and promotes awareness of interference-related issues in wireless communication. By clearly defining its scope and acknowledging its limitations, the study ensures that the tool is used appropriately and effectively within its intended context.

6.6 Software Design

The software design for the Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer integrates open-source tools and custom Python modules to create a comprehensive analysis system. The software architecture separates concerns into distinct modules that communicate through well-defined interfaces.

The Wi-Fi scanner module uses airodump-ng from the aircrack-ng package to perform network discovery. This tool is configured to scan both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands, capturing beacon frames from all detectable access points. Scan results are parsed to extract network properties including SSID, BSSID, channel, signal strength, and encryption type.

The interference detector module processes raw scan data to calculate congestion metrics. It counts networks per channel, weights results by signal strength, and generates numerical interference scores. The module also identifies the least congested channels for recommendation.

The web dashboard module serves the user interface and handles real-time data updates. Built using Flask, the module renders HTML templates and exposes API endpoints for AJAX data requests. The dashboard displays networks in organized lists, renders channel heatmaps using CSS and JavaScript, and presents recommendations in clear formats.

The systemd service configuration manages automatic startup and operation. The service launches the Flask application on system boot, monitors for failures, and restarts the application as needed. This ensures continuous operation without manual intervention.

6.7 Communication Protocol

The communication protocol for the Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer defines how data flows between system components. Understanding these interactions helps in troubleshooting and extending the system.

The TP-Link Wi-Fi adapter communicates with the Raspberry Pi through USB. Monitor mode operation enables passive reception of all wireless frames in the selected channel. Channel hopping allows scanning across multiple frequencies by rapidly switching between channels.

The airodump-ng tool communicates with the wireless adapter through the Linux kernel's netlink interface. It receives parsed frame information and writes results to CSV files and the terminal. Our Python scanner reads these outputs, parsing SSID, BSSID, channel, and signal strength values.

The Flask web server communicates with client browsers using HTTP over TCP/IP. Users access the dashboard through standard web requests. AJAX polling enables real-time updates without page reloads. The server responds with JSON data containing current network and analysis information.

The analyzer can optionally communicate with the local network for latency testing. ICMP ping packets are sent to the router gateway and external servers like 8.8.8.8. Response times are measured and displayed as latency metrics.

6.9 Algorithmic Flow and Data Processing

The algorithmic flow of the Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer defines the sequence of operations for capturing, processing, and displaying wireless data. Understanding this flow helps in comprehending how raw scan data becomes actionable recommendations.

The scanning process begins when the system starts or when the previous scan completes. The Wi-Fi adapter is placed in monitor mode, and airodump-ng is launched to begin capturing beacon frames. The scan runs for a configured duration, typically 15-30 seconds, to capture sufficient data.

Captured frame data is parsed to extract network information. Each detected access point is recorded with its SSID, BSSID, channel, signal strength, and timestamp. Duplicate detections are consolidated, and the most recent signal strength is retained for each network.

The interference analysis algorithm processes the consolidated network data. For each channel, the algorithm counts networks and calculates weighted interference scores based on signal strength. Channels with lower scores are identified as better candidates for Wi-Fi operation.

The recommendation engine analyzes interference scores to suggest optimal channels. It prioritizes non-overlapping channels (1, 6, and 11 for 2.4 GHz) based on their respective congestion levels. Recommendations include the recommended channel and supporting analysis.

6.10 Data Processing Methods

Data processing methods for the Wi-Fi CPE Analyzer involve cleaning, organizing, and analyzing collected network data to extract meaningful metrics. These methods ensure that raw scan data is transformed into useful information.

Network data cleaning removes invalid or duplicate entries. Networks with invalid BSSID values (such as all zeros) are filtered out. Weak signals below the detection threshold are discarded. Entries with missing essential fields are handled appropriately.

Data organization structures network information for efficient access and display. Networks are grouped by frequency band (2.4 GHz and 5 GHz), sorted by signal strength, and indexed by channel. This organization supports rapid dashboard updates and heatmap generation.

Analysis methods calculate interference metrics from organized data. Channel congestion is quantified by counting networks and weighting by signal strength. Overlap analysis identifies networks that share spectrum due to channel proximity. Statistical summaries provide quick assessments of the overall environment.
